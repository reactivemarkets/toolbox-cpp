1 # MoSCoW
2 
3 ## Abstract
4 
5 The MoSCoW method is used to reach a common understanding with stakeholders on the importance they
6 place on the delivery of each requirement.
7 
8 The term MoSCoW itself is an acronym derived from the first letter of each of four prioritisation
9 categories: *Must Have*, *Should Have*, *Could Have*, and *Won't Have*. These words are often
10 capitalized. This document defines these words as they should be interpreted in all design
11 documents.
12 
13 Design documents published to clients should incorporate the following phrase near the beginning of
14 the document:
15 
16 > The key words "MUST", "MUST NOT", "REQUIRED", "SHALL", "SHALL NOT", "SHOULD", "SHOULD NOT",
17 > "RECOMMENDED", "MAY", and "OPTIONAL" in this document are to be interpreted as described in RFC
18 > 2119.
19 
20 ## MUST
21 
22 This word, or the terms *REQUIRED* or *SHALL*, mean that the definition is an absolute requirement
23 of the specification.
24 
25 ## MUST NOT
26 
27 This phrase, or the phrase *SHALL NOT*, mean that the definition is an absolute prohibition of the
28 specification.
29 
30 ## SHOULD
31 
32 This word, or the adjective *RECOMMENDED*, mean that there may exist valid reasons in particular
33 circumstances to ignore a particular item, but the full implications must be understood and
34 carefully weighed before choosing a different course.
35 
36 ## SHOULD NOT
37 
38 This phrase, or the phrase *NOT RECOMMENDED* mean that there may exist valid reasons in particular
39 circumstances when the particular behavior is acceptable or even useful, but the full implications
40 should be understood and the case carefully weighed before implementing any behavior described with
41 this label.
42 
43 ## MAY
44 
45 This word, or the adjective *OPTIONAL*, mean that an item is truly optional. One vendor may choose
46 to include the item because a particular marketplace requires it or because the vendor feels that it
47 enhances the product while another vendor may omit the same item. An implementation which does not
48 include a particular option MUST be prepared to interoperate with another implementation which does
49 include the option, though perhaps with reduced functionality. In the same vein an implementation
50 which does include a particular option MUST be prepared to interoperate with another implementation
51 which does not include the option (except, of course, for the feature the option provides.)
